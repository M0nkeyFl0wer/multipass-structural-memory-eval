Aurora, Colorado voters approved Ballot Question 3A on November 5, 2024, repealing the city's two-decade-old ban on pit bull-type dogs. The measure passed with 56.90% of the vote — 86,168 yes to 65,263 no — with strong margins in Arapahoe County (58,176 yes / 41,369 no) and Adams County (8,450 yes / 3,605 no), though Douglas County tilted slightly against repeal (1,297 yes / 1,759 no). Results were certified by each county's bipartisan canvassing board in early December 2024.

The ballot question asked whether Aurora should allow residents to own American Pit Bull Terriers, American Staffordshire Terriers, and Staffordshire Bull Terriers within city limits. Existing city code Section 14-7, which governs aggressive, potentially dangerous, or dangerous animals, applies to all dogs regardless of breed — meaning the repeal removes breed-specific prohibition while retaining general dangerous-dog rules.

## Legal Path to the Ballot

The 2024 vote was the culmination of a five-year legal dispute. Aurora first banned pit bull-type dogs in 2005, following a mauling incident in which three pit bulls attacked a 10-year-old boy in an Aurora backyard, biting his face and requiring amputation of his left arm. In November 2014, voters were asked whether to repeal the ban and rejected the idea with only 35.6% voting yes — keeping the prohibition in place.

In 2021, Aurora City Council approved an ordinance removing breed restrictions from city code without putting the change to a public vote. Aurora resident Matthew Snider challenged that council action in court, arguing that Aurora's charter requires any ordinance previously referred to voters to be changed only by another electoral vote. In March 2024, 18th Judicial District Court Judge Elizabeth Beebe Volz agreed, ruling the 2021 council repeal void and effectively restoring the ban until voters could decide.

In July 2024, Aurora City Council voted to place Question 3A on the November ballot, referring the repeal decision directly to residents.

## Shelter Community Response

The Dumb Friends League, Colorado's largest animal shelter organization, monitored the ballot question closely. Ali Mickelson, Senior Director of Advocacy and Education at the Dumb Friends League, said the organization "had a big moment of panic" when the court voided the 2021 repeal. Mickelson explained that breed-specific legislation typically creates shelter intake surges: "When there is a breed-specific ban, there is an influx of banned breeds brought to the shelter, and it is very challenging to find them new homes. We have these families that come in, and they're so excited to find out more about a dog, and then they find out that they can't adopt that dog because of restrictions in their place... that just really, really limited our placement for those breeds." Mickelson noted that Colorado shelters maintain a strong network for transferring animals to reduce euthanasia risk.
